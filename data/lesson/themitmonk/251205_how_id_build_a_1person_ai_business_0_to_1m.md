## Summary

The first billion-dollar solo business is coming — the path is **Founder's Triangle (domain + depth + distribution), the DREAM machine (5 core functions), three moats (counterpositioning, switching costs, data loops), and the mindset to choose risk over regret**.

**Why it matters:**
- What cost **$3,000 and days** 2 years ago now costs **$3 and minutes** with AI — a 1,000x cost collapse
- Harvey AI: litigator-founder + DeepMind AI expert + law firm partnerships → reportedly worth **$8 billion** in 2025
- **58% of small businesses** already use generative AI (US Chamber of Commerce)
- Blockbuster said no to buying Netflix for **$50M** → went bankrupt; Netflix now worth **~$500B**
- Cursor tracks developer keystrokes from millions of users → ships a new feature **every single day** via proprietary data loops

**The Four Steps:**

| # | Step | Core Idea | Key Framework |
|---|------|-----------|---------------|
| 1 | Founder's Triangle | Validate your idea before building | Domain (5+ yr expertise) + Depth (craft mastery) + Distribution (unfair customer access) |
| 2 | DREAM Machine | 5 functions that run your business | Demand, Revenue, Engine, Admin, Marketing — automate one task at a time |
| 3 | Moats | Stay in business, not just start one | Counterpositioning, switching costs/habits, proprietary data + learning loops |
| 4 | Mindset | No AI can debug the software in your head | "What will I regret most on my deathbed?" — risks taken and failed < risks failed to take |

**Key Takeaways:**
1. Founder's Triangle: Domain (industry nuance), Depth (what feels like play to you), Distribution (unfair path to customers) — if 1 is green, go; if all 3 are green, floor it
2. DREAM machine: Demand (lead gen), Revenue (pricing/margins), Engine (core product), Admin (back office), Marketing (brand/content) — automate one recurring task this week
3. Three moats: counterpositioning (attack their business model so they can't respond), switching costs (make leaving painful), proprietary data loops (more usage → better product → more usage)
4. "The risks you take and fail have much less impact than the risks you fail to take"
5. Intelligence is now a commodity — your edge is taste, purpose, relationships, judgment, and critical thinking

## Story

Rachel had spent eight years as an intellectual property attorney in Hong Kong, specializing in trademark disputes for consumer brands. She knew exactly which filing mistakes small e-commerce sellers made because she had seen hundreds of cases. One evening, reviewing yet another client's botched trademark application, she thought: "I could build a tool that catches these errors before they happen."

She ran the Founder's Triangle in her head. Domain -- eight years in IP law, she knew the pain points cold. Depth -- she genuinely enjoyed digging through trademark databases, cross-referencing classifications, finding conflicts. It felt like a puzzle, not a chore. Distribution -- she had a WeChat group of 400 e-commerce sellers from past conferences, and they trusted her. All three lights were green. She decided to floor it.

But Rachel had no engineering team, no investors, no office. She was one person with a laptop and an idea. So she built the DREAM machine, one piece at a time.

First, Demand. She messaged twenty sellers from her WeChat group: "Would you pay HK$500 a month for a tool that checks your trademark application for fatal errors before you file?" Fourteen said yes. She had her signal.

Next, Engine. She used AI to build a prototype that scanned a draft application, compared it against existing marks, and flagged likely conflicts. What would have cost her a development team and months of work two years ago took her a weekend and barely any money.

Then Revenue. She set up a simple Stripe payment link -- one price, one click. No complex tiers, no negotiation.

Finally, Admin and Marketing. She automated invoicing and onboarding emails, and started posting short case studies on LinkedIn about common trademark mistakes. Each post drew more sellers who recognized their own problems.

Within three months, Rachel had sixty paying customers. But then a large law firm noticed her tool and started building a copycat. This is where moats saved her. Her counterpositioning was brutal -- the law firm charged HK$5,000 per filing review. If they matched Rachel's HK$500 price, they would cannibalize their own premium service. They could not respond without hurting themselves. Her switching costs grew naturally -- each seller's filing history, custom preferences, and past conflict checks were stored in her system. Moving to the law firm's tool meant starting from scratch. And her data loop kept spinning -- every filing her tool processed made the conflict detection smarter, which attracted more sellers, which generated more data.

Late one night, Rachel stared at her revenue dashboard crossing the million-dollar mark. She thought about her old colleague who had the same idea but never launched because he was waiting for a co-founder and seed funding. He was still waiting. She thought about Blockbuster saying no to Netflix. She thought about the deathbed test -- would she have regretted trying and failing, or never trying at all? The answer was obvious.

**The lesson:** Validate with the Founder's Triangle (domain, depth, distribution), build the DREAM machine one function at a time, defend with moats that competitors cannot copy without hurting themselves, and choose the risk of trying over the certainty of regret.
