## Summary

$650 billion went into AI last year while 1.1 million jobs disappeared — **the AI tsunami is coming and survival depends on getting to "high ground" through the RAIL framework, the 3-layer map, and the 3 Rs (rigor, relationships, resilience)**.

**Why it matters:**
- Shane Legg (DeepMind co-founder): "If your job is 100% done on a laptop, you're competing with AI that costs pennies"
- Price per token dropped **98% in 18 months** — open-source models are 80% as good but way cheaper
- BCG + Harvard study: consultants with deep domain understanding got **40% better results with GPT-4**, those without only 12%
- Open Evidence is worth **$3.5B** and used by **40% of US doctors daily** — built on rigor, not hype
- Microsoft, Amazon, Meta, Google are **cutting non-AI roles to fund AI talent**

**Core Frameworks:**

| Framework | Components | Purpose |
|-----------|-----------|---------|
| **RAIL** | Revenue, Acceleration, In-market, Learning | Detect if you're in the flood zone (no to 2+ = danger) |
| **3-Layer Map** | Infrastructure → Frontier Models → Apps & Services | Know where the money is made (Layer 3 = your opportunity) |
| **3-Step Career Plan** | Audit job → Fire yourself from scripted work → Reinvest in strategic work | Move from flood zone to high ground |
| **3 Rs** | Rigor, Relationships, Resilience | The mindset for long-term survival on high ground |

**The 3-Layer AI Map:**

| Layer | What | Reality | Your Move |
|-------|------|---------|-----------|
| 1. Infrastructure | Chips, cloud, energy | Game of massive capital (Nvidia territory) | Avoid unless sovereign-fund backed |
| 2. Frontier Models | ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini | Consolidating fast, 98% price drop, open-source closing gap | Don't start a me-too model company |
| 3. Apps & Services | Horizontal apps, vertical apps, AI services | Where value is captured | **Build here** — vertical niches, integration services |

**Key Takeaways:**
1. RAIL test: Revenue (real paying customers?), Acceleration (deliver in 2 weeks?), In-market (real users, real data?), Learning (learning from customer use?) — "no" to 2+ means flood zone
2. Layer 3 is where you win: vertical AI apps (deep niche like Cursor for devs), horizontal AI apps (huge market, distribution battle), AI services (implementation + integration at $50K/month)
3. Audit your job: scripted work gets automated, strategic judgment stays human — fire yourself from scripted work before your boss does
4. Deep thinking requires silence, not scrolling; imagination, not information — daydreaming IS deep work
5. Three Rs: Rigor (domain mastery, not surface knowledge), Relationships (trust > resume), Resilience (you're water, not the wave)

## Story

There was a beach town called Seaside where everyone made a living along the shore. Fishermen caught fish by hand, tour guides walked visitors around, surf instructors taught beginners, and a few old-timers ran boat repair shops.

One summer, a massive wave machine appeared offshore. It was built by a giant corporation and it could catch fish automatically, give virtual tours, and teach surfing through holograms -- all for pennies. The fishermen laughed at first. "No machine can do what we do." But within months, the machine was catching ten times the fish at a fraction of the cost. The tour guides noticed fewer customers. The surf instructors saw their classes shrink.

A 35-year-old boat mechanic named Ho watched all of this unfold. He had a wife and a young daughter, and the stakes felt personal. He decided to run what he called a "RAIL check" on his own livelihood. R -- Revenue: Did he have real, paying customers who needed him specifically? Yes, but they were dwindling. A -- Acceleration: Could he deliver a repair in two weeks? Yes, but the machine was learning to do basic repairs too. I -- In-market: Was he getting real data from actual users? Barely -- most of his work was routine. L -- Learning: Was he improving from customer feedback? Not really. He scored "no" on two out of four. He was in the flood zone.

Ho looked at the town and saw three layers. Layer one was the machine itself -- built by a corporation with billions of dollars. He could never compete there. Layer two was the software brains inside the machine -- powerful but commoditizing fast, with cheaper versions popping up every month. Layer three was the services built on top of the machine. That was where the opportunity lived. Ho realized he could become the guy who helped boat owners integrate the wave machine into their fleet -- customizing it, maintaining the connection between old boats and new tech, solving the specific problems that no generic machine could handle.

He audited his week. Half his work was scripted -- the same bolt replacements, the same hull inspections, the same paperwork. He "fired himself" from that work, letting the machine handle the routine stuff. Then he reinvested that freed-up time into strategic work: understanding the wave machine deeply, building trust with boat owners who were confused and scared, and thinking creatively about what the future of Seaside would look like.

Ho also committed to what he called the three Rs. Rigor -- he studied the wave machine inside and out, because a study showed that people with deep domain knowledge got 40% better results using the machine, while surface-level users only got 12%. Relationships -- he became the person boat owners called first, not because of a fancy resume but because they trusted him. Resilience -- he reminded himself that his job title might change, but his skills and character were the ocean beneath the waves. Waves rise and fall. The ocean remains.

Some of the fishermen who laughed at the machine never adapted. They waited too long and found themselves stranded. Ho survived because he saw the flood coming and moved to high ground before the water reached his door.

**The lesson:** When an industry tsunami hits, don't wait to see if it reaches you -- run the RAIL test, build at the application layer where real value lives, fire yourself from scripted work, and invest in the rigor, relationships, and resilience that no machine can replicate.
