## Summary

Five pieces of life-changing advice: **master today (not tomorrow), visible isn't valuable, feedback needs a trust loop, the growth mindset trap, and curiosity as compass** — stop chasing what doesn't matter.

**Why it matters:**
- Deloitte study: **70% of high earners** feel time-starved — you can have money but no time or freedom
- A CEO chased billions, the company collapsed, he walked away with almost nothing — lost all friends except two from college
- Google Slides feedback 48 hours before a board meeting felt like "we've already lost before the game began" — correction without connection sounds like rejection
- Psychologist Kristin Neff: when self-worth depends on being superior, it becomes **fragile** — the growth mindset becomes a treadmill of endless comparison
- "The best opportunities don't knock. They vanish as soon as they appear. But curiosity knows where to find them."

**The Five Lessons:**

| # | Lesson | Core Idea | Key Action |
|---|--------|-----------|------------|
| 1 | Master Today | "Focus on today" — your next role comes from reputation, not resume | Solve what others ignore; do it quietly, skillfully; use all your intelligence HERE |
| 2 | Visible ≠ Valuable | Rich in money, poor in time = prison, not prosperity | Ask: How much time with loved ones? How often do I laugh? How many days do I feel joy? |
| 3 | Trust Loop Feedback | Feedback is medicine — right dose, right time, right system | 5 steps: Face-to-face → Affirm → Clarify → Private → Real conversation |
| 4 | Growth Mindset Trap | Fulfillment is never "one more achievement away" — the goalpost keeps moving | Weekly: What did I learn? What did I improve? What did I smile about? |
| 5 | Curiosity as Compass | Life isn't chess, it's surfing — you don't control the waves | Follow curiosity threads weekly; read outside your field; let randomness surprise you |

**Key Takeaways:**
1. "Be great exactly where your feet are" — the fastest way to your dream job is to master the one you already have; future notices present excellence
2. Once you have what you need, stop chasing more — 70% of high earners are time-starved; visible success (bank balance, titles, cars) isn't real prosperity
3. Trust Loop: face-to-face → affirm real progress → clarify by asking (not assuming) → give feedback in private → have a dialogue, not a monologue — "people don't change because they're corrected, they change because they feel understood"
4. Balance being and becoming — the growth mindset without self-compassion turns into a hamster wheel that just gets shinier
5. Curiosity is a whisper — your mind has to be quiet enough to hear it; the best opportunities don't announce themselves

## Story

There was a small pizza shop in Mong Kok called Golden Slice. The owner, Uncle Keung, was 36, a former engineer who had left his corporate job to open the shop with his wife. For the first two years, he obsessed over one thing: making the best margherita on the block. Not expansion. Not franchising. Not Instagram fame. Just the dough, the sauce, the oven temperature. He fixed the one problem every pizza place in the neighborhood ignored -- inconsistent crust -- and he did it quietly, batch after batch, until regulars started lining up before he even flipped the "Open" sign.

That was Lesson One playing out in real life: master today. Uncle Keung's reputation spread not because of a flashy marketing campaign, but because his pizza was excellent right where his feet were.

One evening, a food blogger offered him a deal: paid sponsorship, viral exposure, potential to open three more locations within a year. Uncle Keung almost said yes. But then he sat down with his wife at their tiny kitchen table and asked three questions. How many dinners had they eaten together that week? How often had their daughter laughed with them? How many days had he felt genuine joy? The answers were sobering. He was already drifting toward being rich in revenue but poor in time -- the same trap that swallows 70 percent of high earners. He turned down the deal.

A few months later, his young apprentice, Ah Wai, started burning pizzas. Uncle Keung's first instinct was to call him out in front of the kitchen staff. But he remembered something an old mentor once told him: correction without connection sounds like rejection. So instead, he pulled Ah Wai aside after closing. He started by pointing out how much Ah Wai's dough-stretching had improved. Then he asked, "What's going on with the oven timing? Walk me through your thinking." It turned out Ah Wai had been experimenting with a higher temperature to speed things up -- good intention, wrong execution. They worked out a fix together over tea. That was the Trust Loop: face-to-face, affirm, clarify by asking, keep it private, make it a dialogue.

Meanwhile, Uncle Keung noticed something in himself. Even though Golden Slice was thriving, he kept setting new targets -- a catering arm, a cookbook, a second location. Each time he hit a goal, the goalpost moved. He was on a hamster wheel that just kept getting shinier. One Friday, he forced himself to write down three things: what he learned that week, what he improved, and what made him smile. The "smile" column was blank. That was his wake-up call. He realized he needed to balance becoming a better business owner with simply being a present father and husband.

The final shift came unexpectedly. Uncle Keung picked up a magazine about fermentation science -- completely outside his usual reading. One article on sourdough starters sparked an idea for a 72-hour cold-fermented crust that became Golden Slice's signature. He never would have found it if he had been too busy chasing the next business milestone to let his curiosity wander. Life, he realized, is not chess. It is surfing. You do not control the waves. But if your mind is quiet enough, curiosity whispers where to paddle next.

**The lesson:** Stop chasing what doesn't matter -- master where you are, measure time over money, build trust before giving feedback, balance being with becoming, and let curiosity lead when plans run dry.
