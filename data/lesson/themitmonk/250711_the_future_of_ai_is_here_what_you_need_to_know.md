## Summary

The OpenAI saga in three acts — **the 120-hour implosion, the original sin (nonprofit → for-profit), and Sam Altman vs everyone** — revealing three timeless lessons: relationships > resumes, skills > roles, and clear communication prevents crises.

**Why it matters:**
- Sam Altman was fired via Zoom call and **regained control within 120 hours** — not through legal power, but through relationships
- **700 of 770 employees** signed a letter demanding the board resign — including Ilya Sutskever who had fired Altman
- OpenAI's "original sin": a nonprofit that found a **gold mine** and created a capped-profit subsidiary — then Microsoft invested **$13B** for exclusive access
- By May 2025: **800M–1B weekly users**, 92% of Fortune 500 on ChatGPT, valued at $300B — still projected to **lose $9B** despite $13B revenue
- GPT-3 scaled to **175B parameters** (100x over predecessor) — like going from a go-kart to a rocket ship; only possible with Microsoft's compute

**The Three Acts:**

| Act | What Happened | Key Tension |
|-----|--------------|-------------|
| 1: 120-Hour Implosion | Board fires Altman on Zoom; 700/770 employees revolt; Altman returns as CEO in 5 days | Board said "destruction of the company could be consistent with the mission" |
| 2: Original Sin | 2015 nonprofit → 2019 capped-profit subsidiary; Microsoft invests $13B; code goes closed | Mission vs money: "the open in OpenAI" died when commercial reality hit |
| 3: Sam vs Everyone | Altman withheld info (ChatGPT launch, Middle East chip talks); safety team departed | Board watched silently for months, then acted like a coup instead of a course correction |

**Three Lessons:**

| # | Lesson | Core Idea | Key Action |
|---|--------|-----------|------------|
| 1 | Build Relationships Before You Need Them | Altman survived because of people (Nadella, employees), not resume or legal power | Help others without keeping score; build a handful of real supporters who see your potential |
| 2 | Roles Don't Last, Skills Do | Altman, Jobs, Dorsey all ousted from companies they started — no one is safe | Save 6-12 months runway; experiment with AI extending your work; keep building |
| 3 | Clear Communication Prevents Crises | Board observed silently for months → acted suddenly → looked like a coup | Have uncomfortable conversations early, clearly, with care and compassion; read "Crucial Conversations" |

**Key Takeaways:**
1. The biggest failure at OpenAI wasn't technical — it was human: relationships misfiring, silence instead of feedback, a board without a backup plan
2. Altman's strategic move: no equity = moral authority; "the one with nothing to lose" had more power than any shareholder
3. The transformer (Google, 2017) → GPT-1 → GPT-3 (175B params) → ChatGPT — OpenAI took Google's architecture and built the fastest-growing product in tech history
4. Open vs closed AI war: Meta/DeepSeek say transparency = safety; OpenAI/Google say closed = controlled safety — both have trade-offs
5. "Even the world's top experts don't know how this movie ends. Don't be threatened. Don't be intimidated. Keep building."

## Story

Imagine a high school science club that started with a noble mission: build a solar-powered water purifier and give the designs away for free so any village in the world could use them. Five students founded it, and they wrote a charter that said, "This club exists to help humanity, not to win trophies."

The club was brilliant but broke. They could not afford the materials to scale their prototype. Then the school's biggest sponsor, a tech company called MegaCorp, offered a deal: we will fund your entire lab -- equipment, materials, everything -- but in exchange, we get exclusive access to your designs first. The founders argued for weeks. Eventually they agreed, reasoning that a funded project helping some people was better than an unfunded project helping nobody. They rewrote the charter. The "open" part of "Open Science Club" quietly died.

The prototype worked beautifully. The club's purifier became the most talked-about project in the district. Other schools wanted to copy it. MegaCorp poured more money in. The club grew from 5 members to 770.

But the five founders started to fracture. The club president, Sam, had a habit of making big decisions without telling the rest of the board -- launching a public demo without warning, negotiating with a rival sponsor behind closed doors. The board noticed, said nothing for months, and then one Friday afternoon, fired Sam over a video call. No warning. No conversation. No backup plan for who would lead next.

What happened next shocked everyone. Within hours, 700 of the 770 members signed a letter demanding the board step down. Even one of the board members who had voted to fire Sam changed his mind. MegaCorp's representative called Sam directly and said, "If the board will not take you back, come work for us and bring everyone with you." By Tuesday -- five days later -- Sam was back as president.

Sam survived not because of any rule in the charter. He survived because of relationships. He had spent years helping other members, checking in on them, connecting them with opportunities. When the crisis hit, those people showed up for him. The person with the strongest resume on the board had no such network. Legal power meant nothing against 700 people who trusted someone else.

The whole mess also revealed a second lesson. Sam, like every founder, was not permanent. Founders of the biggest clubs in the district had all been removed at some point -- it happened everywhere. The members who thrived were the ones who kept building transferable skills regardless of who sat in the president's chair. They saved up resources, stayed versatile, and never assumed their role was guaranteed.

And the third lesson was the simplest. The board had watched Sam's behavior for months, growing uneasy, saying nothing. Then they acted suddenly, and it looked like a betrayal instead of a course correction. If they had pulled Sam aside early and said, "Here is what we are seeing, here is what concerns us, let us talk about it," the entire crisis could have been a conversation instead of an explosion.

**The lesson:** Build relationships before you need them, because trust outweighs titles when a crisis hits; never assume your role is permanent, so keep sharpening skills that travel with you; and have uncomfortable conversations early and clearly, because silence followed by sudden action looks like a coup, not a correction.
