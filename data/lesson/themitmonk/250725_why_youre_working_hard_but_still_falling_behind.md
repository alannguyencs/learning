## Summary

Seven high-income habits inspired by legendary minds: **harness anxiety (Jensen Huang), build anti-fragility (Nassim Taleb), psychological leverage (Disraeli), metacognition (Munger), decision velocity (Bezos), productive failure (Federer), and craft with care (Jony Ive)**.

**Why it matters:**
- Jensen Huang (Nvidia, $3T+) is fueled almost entirely by **negative emotions like anxiety** — he channels it, not suppresses it
- Yerkes-Dodson Law: optimal cognitive performance lives in the zone between **extreme panic and extreme boredom** — that's where high earners operate
- Meta-analysis: metacognition is a **better predictor of performance than IQ** in complex (high-income) roles
- Jeff Bezos 70% rule: most decisions are **two-way doors** — the cost of delay is often greater than the cost of mistakes
- Roger Federer lost **46% of all points** in 1,500+ matches — but he's the GOAT because he recommits instantly after every loss

**The Seven Habits:**

| # | Habit | Inspired By | Core Idea | Key Action |
|---|-------|------------|-----------|------------|
| 1 | Harness Anxiety | Jensen Huang | Anxiety is a wake-up alarm, not a fire alarm | Rehearse under pressure; label anxiety early; track anxiety-income correlation |
| 2 | Anti-Fragility | Nassim Taleb | Systems that get stronger FROM stress | Seek manageable stress; add controlled chaos; create optionality (Plan A/B/C) |
| 3 | Psychological Leverage | Disraeli | Make others feel like the smartest in the room | Map humans, not org charts; be curious by default; learn cognitive biases |
| 4 | Metacognition | Charlie Munger | Think about your thinking; know the other side's argument better than they do | Ask "What made me think that?"; explain thinking out loud; investigate assumptions |
| 5 | Decision Velocity | Jeff Bezos | Decide at 70% confidence; most doors are two-way | Label the door (one-way vs two-way); act at 70%; make small decisions fast |
| 6 | Productive Failure | Roger Federer | Lost 46% of points but is the GOAT — it's just a point | Separate outcome from process; recommit fast after mistakes; reps over gifts |
| 7 | Craft with Care | Jony Ive | "What we make defines who we are" — output is an act of giving | Set intention before execution; express care through details; find joy in work |

**Key Takeaways:**
1. Anxiety-income equation = risk-reward equation — high income and high pressure travel together; master the second to get the first
2. Anti-fragile > resilient: fragile breaks (glass), robust resists (rock), anti-fragile gets stronger from stress (muscle, immune system) — build optionality and controlled chaos
3. Neural coupling: when you show genuine curiosity about someone, their brain literally syncs with yours — attention is the most important social currency
4. Munger's metacognition: "I never allow myself to have an opinion without knowing the other side's argument better than they do" — beats IQ for predicting performance
5. All seven connect: anxiety → energy, anti-fragility → fuel from chaos, leverage → understand others, metacognition → optimize thinking, velocity → faster learning, failure → growth, care → meaning

## Story

Mei was a thirty-five-year-old engineer at a startup in Kwun Tong. She worked harder than anyone on her floor -- first one in, last one out, laptop open on the MTR ride home. Yet somehow, colleagues who seemed to work less kept getting promoted, landing bigger projects, and earning more. One day she sat in the pantry staring at her cold lunch and thought, "What am I missing?"

Over the next few months, she figured it out -- not one thing, but seven.

The first clue came from her CEO, who confessed during an all-hands meeting that he felt anxious before every board presentation. "I used to think anxiety meant I was not ready," he said. "Now I know it means I care enough to prepare harder." Mei realized she had been trying to eliminate her nervousness before big demos. But the nervousness was not a fire alarm telling her to flee. It was a wake-up alarm telling her to rehearse one more time. There was a sweet spot between total panic and total boredom where her brain worked best. She started labeling her anxiety out loud -- "I am nervous, and I am channeling it" -- and her demo performance improved overnight.

The second clue came when the startup's server went down during a product launch. Everyone panicked except the DevOps lead, who calmly said, "Good. Now we know exactly where the weak point is." He explained that he deliberately ran stress tests every quarter -- small, controlled failures -- because systems that experience stress in manageable doses get stronger, not weaker. Glass shatters under pressure. A rock resists but stays the same. A muscle tears, repairs, and comes back stronger. Mei started volunteering for the hardest debugging tasks and keeping a Plan B and Plan C for every project. She was not just surviving chaos; she was growing from it.

The third clue came at a company dinner. Mei noticed that the head of sales never talked about himself. He asked questions -- "What got you into engineering?" "What is the hardest problem you solved this year?" -- and everyone at the table lit up. People left the dinner feeling brilliant, and they all wanted to work with him. Mei learned that attention is the most valuable social currency. When you make someone else feel like the smartest person in the room, their brain literally syncs with yours. She started mapping what people cared about and feared, not just their job titles on the org chart.

The fourth clue came from a book her father gave her. It quoted an old investor who said, "I never allow myself to hold an opinion without knowing the other side's argument better than they do." Mei tried this at her next architecture review. Before proposing her design, she argued against it -- out loud, in front of the team. She found two blind spots she would have missed. Research showed that this skill, thinking about your own thinking, predicted performance in complex roles better than raw IQ.

The fifth clue came when a product decision stalled for three weeks because nobody wanted to commit without more data. Mei's manager finally said, "Is this a one-way door or a two-way door? If we can reverse it, let us decide at 70 percent confidence and move." Most decisions, it turned out, were two-way doors. The cost of waiting was almost always higher than the cost of being wrong.

The sixth clue came from sports. Mei's daughter played badminton and lost a tournament match by two points. She was devastated. Mei told her, "The greatest tennis player in history lost 46 percent of all the points he ever played. Forty-six percent. But he won because after every lost point, he fully committed to the next one." Mei realized she had been carrying her own lost points -- a failed prototype, a rejected proposal -- like stones in her pockets. She started separating outcomes from process: a bad result did not mean a bad approach. Extract the lesson, drop the stone, recommit.

The seventh clue came from the simplest place. Mei noticed that she rushed through her code reviews, treating them as a chore to finish. Then she watched a documentary about a designer who said, "What we make defines who we are." She started setting an intention before every piece of work: "What am I trying to express through this?" She found joy in the details -- clean variable names, elegant error handling, thoughtful comments. Her code reviews went from something she endured to something she was proud of. The care showed, even in parts nobody else would see.

Months later, Mei was not working more hours. She was working with more intention. The seven habits formed a single system: anxiety gave her energy, stress made her stronger, curiosity connected her to people, metacognition sharpened her thinking, speed created learning cycles, failure accelerated growth, and care made the whole thing meaningful.

**The lesson:** Working hard is not enough -- harness anxiety as fuel, grow stronger from stress, win people through genuine curiosity, think about your own thinking, decide fast on reversible choices, recommit instantly after failure, and pour care into your craft, because these seven habits are the system that separates falling behind from pulling ahead.
