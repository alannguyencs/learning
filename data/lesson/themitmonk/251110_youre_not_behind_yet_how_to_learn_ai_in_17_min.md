## Summary

Master AI in 30 days with a **7-step roadmap: Machine English (AIM), pick one instrument, build context (MAAP), debug your thinking (3 patterns), steer to experts, verify claims (5 methods), and develop taste (OCEAN)**.

**Why it matters:**
- AI doesn't understand language — it **predicts** the next token from probability and proximity in a mathematical embedding space
- Vague prompts produce vague guesses; sharp prompts produce sharp guesses — that's "machine English"
- Drummers pick up guitar faster than beginners (Frontiers in Psychology) — mastering one AI tool transfers to all others
- AI sounds **equally confident** when wrong as when right — verification is non-negotiable
- Every prompt you write trains the model AND trains you — "AI is not here to replace human work, it's here to restore human worth"

**The 7-Step 30-Day Roadmap:**

| Week | Step | What | Key Framework |
|------|------|------|---------------|
| 1 | Machine English | Learn how AI actually works (tokens, embeddings, next-token prediction) | AIM: Actor, Input, Mission |
| 1 | Pick One Instrument | Go deep on one model (ChatGPT, Gemini, or Claude) | Like learning drums → guitar transfers |
| 2 | Build Context | Feed AI the map it needs to navigate its mathematical space | MAAP: Memory, Assets, Actions, Prompt |
| 2 | Debug Your Thinking | When output is weak, the fault is yours — iterate | 3 patterns: Chain of Thought, Verifier, Refinement |
| 3 | Steer to Experts | Pull AI from mediocrity toward expert-level depth | Name experts/frameworks to navigate AI's sharper edges |
| 3 | Verify Claims | AI hallucinates by design — separate intelligence from illusion | 5 checks: Assumptions, Sources, Counter-evidence, Auditing, Cross-model |
| 4 | Develop Taste | Turn generic output into something that sounds like YOU | OCEAN: Original, Concrete, Evident, Assertive, Narrative |

**Key Takeaways:**
1. AIM framework: Actor (persona), Input (context/data), Mission (what to do) — turns prompts into structures AI can compute
2. MAAP for context: Memory (conversation history), Assets (files/data), Actions (tools AI can call), Prompt (instruction) — richer context = better reasoning
3. Three debug patterns: Chain of Thought ("think step by step"), Verifier ("ask me 3 clarifying questions"), Refinement ("propose 2 sharper versions of my question")
4. Five verification methods: list assumptions with confidence ranking, cite sources with URLs, find counter-evidence, recompute figures, run same prompt across models
5. OCEAN for taste: Original (non-obvious angles), Concrete (names, examples, numbers), Evident (show reasoning), Assertive (pick a side), Narrative (tell a story)

## Story

Jason was a mid-career product manager at a Hong Kong hardware company. He kept hearing that AI would change everything, but every time he opened ChatGPT, he typed something vague like "give me marketing ideas" and got back a wall of generic bullet points. He felt like he was falling behind while everyone else had figured it out. Then his colleague Priya, who had spent a month seriously learning AI, showed him what he was doing wrong.

"You are speaking human to a machine," Priya said. "AI does not understand language. It predicts the next most likely word based on the words you already gave it. Vague input, vague prediction. Sharp input, sharp prediction." She taught him Machine English -- the idea that prompts are not conversations, they are instructions that navigate a mathematical space. The sharper the coordinates, the better the destination.

She showed him the AIM framework. Every prompt needs three things: an Actor (who should the AI pretend to be), an Input (what context and data does it need), and a Mission (what does "done" look like). Jason tried it immediately. Instead of "give me marketing ideas," he wrote: "You are a product marketing manager at a consumer electronics company in Asia. Here is our product spec sheet and our competitor's latest press release. Write three positioning statements that emphasize our battery life advantage, each under 30 words." The output was night and day. Same AI tool, completely different result.

Next, Priya told him to pick one model and go deep. "You play guitar, right?" she asked. Jason nodded. "Remember how learning guitar made it easier to pick up ukulele later? Same thing. Master one AI model's rhythm -- learn where it is strong, where it hallucinates, what kind of follow-ups sharpen the output. That skill transfers to every other model." Jason chose Claude and spent two weeks using it for every task: drafting emails, summarizing meeting notes, brainstorming feature names. By the end of those two weeks, he could feel when a response was going to be weak before he even finished reading it.

In week two, Priya introduced context-building with MAAP. "AI is like a taxi driver in a city it has never visited," she explained. "You need to hand it a map." Memory meant feeding the conversation history so it remembered earlier decisions. Assets meant uploading actual files -- the product spec, the sales data, the customer interview transcript. Actions meant enabling tools the AI could call, like a calculator or a code runner. Prompt meant sharpening the instruction itself. Jason started uploading his actual project documents before asking questions. The answers went from "plausible guesses" to "genuinely useful analysis."

But AI still got things wrong. Confidently wrong. Jason asked it to estimate market size for a new product category and it returned a number with two citations. Both citations were fabricated -- the reports did not exist. Priya was not surprised. "AI hallucinates by design. It is a prediction engine, not a truth engine. You have to verify." She taught him five checks: ask the AI to list its assumptions with a confidence ranking, demand sources with URLs, request counter-evidence to its own claims, recompute any figures it generated, and run the same prompt across a second model to see if the answers agree. Jason made this his habit. It added five minutes to each session and saved him from embarrassing mistakes in three presentations that month.

The last piece was taste. Jason noticed that even when AI gave correct answers, they sounded generic -- like a textbook written by committee. Priya showed him the OCEAN framework: make the output Original (non-obvious angles), Concrete (specific names and numbers, not vague generalities), Evident (show the reasoning, not just the conclusion), Assertive (pick a position instead of hedging), and Narrative (tell a story, not a list). Jason started adding "write this as if you have a strong opinion and are willing to defend it" to his prompts. The outputs started sounding like something he would actually send under his own name.

A month in, Jason was not an AI expert. But he was no longer behind. He could write prompts that returned useful results on the first try, he could spot hallucinations before they reached a slide deck, and his outputs had a voice that sounded like his own instead of a machine's.

**The lesson:** AI does not understand you -- it predicts you. Learn to speak its language with structure (AIM), feed it real context (MAAP), debug weak output by iterating your thinking, verify everything it says, and develop your own taste so the output sounds like a person, not a vending machine.
