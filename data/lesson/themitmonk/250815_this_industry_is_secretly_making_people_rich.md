## Summary

The **$236B super agent economy** is the next massive wealth creation wave — understand the CRAFT framework, one-person kingdoms, and the dark side of job displacement before it's too late.

**Why it matters:**
- AI agent economy projected at **$236 billion** — OpenAI alone expects $30B in revenue from agents in 4 years
- Only **19% of companies** are piloting AI agents; 81% are still in "slide decks and strategy offsites" — this is ground floor
- AlphaZero: went from knowing **nothing about chess to beating grandmasters** in under 24 hours — that's the speed of AI agent evolution
- Parloa: AI agents now handle **80%+ of customer support calls**; Direct Mortgage: cut loan approval time and cost by **80%**
- Kal AI: 18-year-old built a nutrition app with **5 million downloads** and projecting $30M revenue with just 15 employees — one-person kingdoms are real

**Key Takeaways:**
1. AI agents are not chatbots — they plan, decide, and execute tasks autonomously by connecting to apps, sites, and tools; think Waymo cabs for knowledge work
2. CRAFT: agents learn fast when work is repeatable and verifiable; the more clearly you define a process, the easier to hand it to an agent
3. One-person kingdoms: scaling now means wiring AI agents, not hiring employees — limitation is imagination, not resources
4. Move from drafting prompts to delegating tasks — automate one task this weekend
5. Invest in human skills (relationship building, empathy, storytelling, critical thinking) — and practice wabi-sabi: beauty in imperfection

## Story

Leon ran a two-person trademark detection consultancy out of a co-working space in Lai Chi Kok. His partner handled sales; Leon handled everything else -- client emails, research reports, patent database searches, invoice processing, and the actual computer vision analysis that was their core service. He worked fourteen-hour days and still could not take on more than eight clients a month. He needed to hire, but hiring meant overhead, office space, HR headaches, and months of training. He was stuck.

Then one weekend, Leon's university classmate, a machine learning engineer at a big firm, showed him something that changed his thinking entirely. She had set up an AI agent -- not a chatbot, not a fancy search bar, but an autonomous digital worker -- that handled her team's internal communications. It read incoming emails, drafted responses, routed urgent ones to humans, and scheduled follow-up meetings. It did not just suggest; it decided and acted. "Think of it as a Waymo cab for knowledge work," she said. "You do not tell it every turn. You give it a destination, and it drives."

Leon was skeptical but curious. He started by auditing his own week using a simple framework: every task fell into one of five buckets. Communication -- all his client emails, scheduling, and status updates. Research -- scanning trademark databases and legal filings. Analytics -- generating reports on brand similarity scores. Functional -- invoicing, contract renewals, deadline reminders. Technical -- running and fine-tuning his detection models. He wrote the letters C-R-A-F-T on a whiteboard and tagged every task.

Then he asked himself one question for each task: "Is this repeatable and verifiable?" If the answer was yes, it was a candidate for an agent. Client email triage? Repeatable, verifiable. Patent database scanning? Repeatable, verifiable. Invoice generation? Repeatable, verifiable. The actual judgment call on whether two trademarks were confusingly similar? That required human nuance, context, and empathy for the client's situation. That stayed with Leon.

He started small. That same weekend, he wired an agent to handle his Communication bucket -- sorting incoming emails, drafting first responses, and flagging anything that needed his personal attention. The following week, he added a Research agent that scanned five trademark databases nightly and produced a summary report by 7 a.m. Within a month, he had agents covering four of the five CRAFT categories. His workload dropped by roughly 60 percent. He did not hire a single person.

Leon's partner was stunned. "We went from eight clients a month to twenty-two," she said. "And you are working fewer hours." Leon had accidentally built what some people were calling a one-person kingdom. An eighteen-year-old in another industry had done the same thing with a nutrition app -- five million downloads, projecting thirty million in revenue, only fifteen employees. The constraint was no longer money or headcount. It was imagination.

But Leon also saw the dark side. A large consultancy in his space announced a major AI investment the same week it laid off a quarter of its staff. Some roles were simply going away. The people who thrived were the ones who moved into new positions -- AI workflow designers, trust-and-safety reviewers, client relationship leads -- roles that required judgment, empathy, and storytelling. The agents took the boring, repeatable work. The humans kept the ambiguous, relational work.

Leon pinned a note above his monitor: "The fastest way to scale is to shrink your to-do list." Below it he added a reminder about wabi-sabi, the Japanese idea of beauty in imperfection. The agents were not perfect. The outputs needed human polish. But that imperfection was fine, because the human touch was what made the final product trustworthy.

Eighty-one percent of companies had not even started experimenting with agents. Leon was on the ground floor of a two-hundred-thirty-six-billion-dollar wave, and he got there by wiring one task on a single weekend.

**The lesson:** AI agents are not chatbots -- they are autonomous workers that decide and act; use the CRAFT framework to audit your tasks, delegate the repeatable and verifiable ones, keep the human judgment for yourself, and start building your one-person kingdom this weekend, because 81 percent of the world has not started yet and the ground floor will not last forever.
