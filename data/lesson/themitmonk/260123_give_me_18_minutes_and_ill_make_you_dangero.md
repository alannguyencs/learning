## Summary

The top 1% use AI backwards — **not to get answers, but to train their brain through two zones (lazy on capped tasks, obsessed on uncapped ones), four prompting camps, progressive mental overload, and the courage to play the fool**.

**Why it matters:**
- Harvard Business Review: CEOs waste **72% of their time** in meetings that don't move the needle
- Completion bias makes us treat all tasks as equal — we get the same dopamine from formatting slides as from million-dollar strategy work
- Herbert Simon (Nobel Prize): **satisficing** — stop when it's good enough for capped-payoff tasks
- Astronauts lose up to **20% muscle and bone mass** in zero gravity — AI is zero gravity for thinking
- Microsoft went from **$300B to $3T** after Satya Nadella's one cultural shift: know-it-alls → learn-it-alls

**The Four-Step Framework:**

| # | Step | Core Idea | Key Framework |
|---|------|-----------|---------------|
| 1 | Intelligent Laziness | Two curves: capped payoff (be lazy) vs uncapped payoff (obsess) | DRAG: Drafting, Research, Analysis, Grunt work |
| 2 | Intelligent Hill | Stop treating AI like a calculator — it's a probability engine | 4 camps: zero-shot → one-shot → few-shot → chain-of-thought → agents |
| 3 | Intelligent Gym | For transformation tasks, use AI to ADD friction, not remove it | Progressive overload: high school → college → executive → hostile boss |
| 4 | Intelligent Fool | Ego is the obstacle; mastery requires beginner's mind | Ask the questions you'd never ask colleagues — AI doesn't roll its eyes |

**Key Takeaways:**
1. Two curves: capped-payoff tasks (formatting, emails, reports) → satisfice and use DRAG to outsource to AI; uncapped-payoff tasks (product design, relationships, pricing) → obsess with your full human capacity
2. DRAG framework: Drafting (zero-to-one), Research (deep research), Analysis (pattern-finding), Grunt work (reformatting, cleaning) — apply ONLY on zone-one capped tasks
3. Climb the Intelligent Hill: zero-shot (rolling dice) → one-shot (one example) → few-shot (patterns from multiple examples) → chain-of-thought (show your work) → agents (multi-step autonomous)
4. Intelligent Gym: AI as spotter, not wheelchair. Progressive overload: quiz at high school → college → executive interview → hostile boss level. Growth happens at the edge of your ability, through errors and discomfort
5. Intelligent Fool: "The biggest benefit of intelligence is not the end of ignorance — it's the end of pretending." Ask basic questions. Simplify 3x. Feel ridiculous. That's learning.

## Story

Kevin ran a small pizza shop in Mong Kok. He had one employee, Wai, and together they handled everything -- dough, toppings, deliveries, bookkeeping, customer complaints, the works.

One day Kevin bought a fancy new pizza oven that could do almost anything. It could shape dough, monitor baking temperatures, even suggest topping combinations. Kevin was thrilled. He started using the oven for everything. He let it decide the menu. He let it handle customer orders. He let it write the shop's marketing posts. Within a month, Kevin realized something disturbing: he had forgotten how to taste-test his own pizza. His palate had gone soft. He was the owner of a pizza shop who could no longer tell good pizza from bad pizza.

His friend Mei, who ran a noodle shop down the street, took a different approach. She split her work into two piles every morning. Pile one was "capped" stuff -- tasks where good enough was good enough. Restocking napkins, updating the menu board, replying to supplier emails. She let her new kitchen assistant handle all of that. She called it her DRAG list: Drafting menus, Researching suppliers, Analyzing sales data, Grunt work like cleaning spreadsheets. Pile two was "uncapped" stuff -- tasks where her human judgment was irreplaceable. Developing new recipes, building relationships with regulars, deciding the pricing strategy that kept her shop alive in a neighborhood full of competitors. On these, she poured every ounce of her attention.

But Mei did something else that Kevin never thought of. She used the kitchen assistant as a trainer, not a crutch. When she invented a new broth recipe, she would ask the assistant to quiz her on it -- first easy questions a high schooler could answer, then tougher ones like a college food science exam, then brutal questions a Michelin inspector might throw at her. She called it her "gym routine." The assistant was her spotter, not her wheelchair. She got stronger because the assistant added friction to her learning, not comfort.

Mei also climbed what she called her "prompting hill." At first, she gave the assistant vague instructions -- "help me with the menu" -- and got vague results. Then she learned to give one example of what she wanted. Then multiple examples with patterns. Eventually, she was giving the assistant step-by-step reasoning chains and letting it handle multi-step research on its own. Her results got dramatically better at each level.

And whenever Mei felt embarrassed about asking a basic question -- "wait, what actually IS MSG?" -- she reminded herself that asking dumb questions was the fastest path to real understanding. The assistant never rolled its eyes. The assistant never judged. She played the fool on purpose, because pretending to know something was the most expensive mistake in the kitchen.

Kevin eventually came around. He stopped using the oven as a replacement for his brain and started using it the way Mei used her assistant -- lazy on the stuff that didn't matter, obsessed on the stuff that did, and always training himself harder.

**The lesson:** Use AI to eliminate busywork and add friction to your thinking -- be lazy on capped tasks, obsessed on uncapped ones, and never let the tool replace the reps that keep your mind sharp.
