## Summary

Top performers don't manage time — they manage attention through the **3-2-1 system: three roles (Maker, Marker, Multiplier), two zones (wartime, peacetime), and one non-negotiable that only you can do**.

**Why it matters:**
- Jack Dorsey ran Twitter and Square simultaneously — same 168 hours as everyone else, different attention allocation
- Brian Chesky nearly killed Airbnb by delegating too much in wartime — had to shift from multiplier back to maker, delivered **500+ product improvements in 3 years**
- Marshall Goldsmith: "What got you here won't get you there" — habits that work in simple phases actively harm you in complex roles
- Eric Schmidt: a CEO's job is to be "the most expensive router" — connecting, not creating
- Steve Jobs and Jony Ive met daily for lunch — deep involvement without micromanagement because it was partnership, not control

**The 3-2-1 System:**

| Component | What | Key Insight |
|-----------|------|-------------|
| **3 Roles** | Maker (do it yourself), Marker (edit/delegate), Multiplier (orchestrate/align) | If you don't know your role, you'll manage time the wrong way |
| **2 Zones** | Wartime (crisis, roll up sleeves) vs Peacetime (stable, step back) | Your zone dictates your role — wartime can force multiplier → maker |
| **1 Non-negotiable** | "What is the one thing that ONLY I can do?" | Delegate everything else; focus proactive energy on this one thing |

**Key Takeaways:**
1. Three roles: Maker (IC, deep work, clear deliverables), Marker (edit others' work, build processes, delegate + do), Multiplier (recruit, orchestrate, route, big picture) — know which one you're in
2. Two zones: Wartime forces you down the role ladder (multiplier → marker → maker); peacetime lets you step back up — Chesky's Airbnb recovery proves dynamic role-switching works
3. One non-negotiable: ask "What can ONLY I do?" — for a CEO it might be mission-driven relationships; for you it might be the one skill or activity nobody else can replicate
4. Trust management > time management: new teammates → work alongside (comfort), experienced → guide closely (clarity), experts → step in to unblock (context)
5. "You cannot control time. You can only steward the time you have."

## Story

Vincent ran a small dim sum restaurant in Mong Kok. For years he did everything himself -- folded dumplings at 4 AM, served tables at lunch, balanced the books at midnight. He was the ultimate Maker. Business was steady, and his hands touched every dish.

Then something changed. A food blogger featured his har gow, and suddenly there were lines around the block. Vincent hired three cooks and a cashier. But he kept folding dumplings himself. He kept running to the register between batches. Orders piled up. Cooks stood idle waiting for his instructions. Customers walked away. Revenue actually dropped despite the attention. Vincent was stuck in Maker mode when the situation demanded he become a Marker -- someone who trains the cooks, reviews their work, and sets standards so the kitchen runs without him at every station.

His wife, a project manager at a tech company, watched this unfold and sat him down one Sunday. "You have three roles," she said. "Maker is when you fold the dumplings. Marker is when you taste the new cook's dumplings and tell him what to fix. Multiplier is when you hire a head chef, set the menu direction, and go find the next restaurant location. Right now you need to be a Marker, but you are acting like a Maker."

Vincent protested. "But nobody folds them like I do."

She handed him a second question: "Are we in wartime or peacetime?" The answer was obvious -- they were in wartime. Lines out the door, kitchen overwhelmed, staff confused. In wartime, yes, sometimes you drop back down and fold dumplings during the rush. But only for the rush. The rest of the day, you train.

Then she asked the question that changed everything: "What is the one thing that only you can do?" Vincent thought about it. Anyone could learn to fold dumplings -- he could teach that in a week. But only he knew the secret broth recipe his grandmother taught him. Only he could taste a batch and know if it was right. Only he had the relationships with the specialty suppliers in the New Territories. That was his non-negotiable.

So Vincent restructured his days. Mornings: he trained the new cooks side by side, working alongside them until they were comfortable. Afternoons: he gave his experienced cook clear standards -- "the wrapper should be translucent, exactly seven pleats, this much filling." For his most senior cook, he simply said, "You run the evening shift. Call me only if we run out of shrimp." Different levels of trust, different levels of delegation.

Within two months the kitchen ran smoothly without Vincent at every station. He spent his best hours on the broth, the supplier relationships, and planning a second location. Revenue tripled.

**The lesson:** Time management is really attention management -- know your role (Maker, Marker, or Multiplier), read whether you are in wartime or peacetime, protect the one thing only you can do, and delegate everything else based on trust.
