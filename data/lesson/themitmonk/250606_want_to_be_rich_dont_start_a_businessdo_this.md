## Summary

Seven moves to build wealth as an employee: **pick the right industry, choose specialist vs generalist path, RPM performance engine, get close to the customer, make asymmetric bets, master financial principles, and meditate to monetize**.

**Why it matters:**
- Georgetown Center of Education: industry choice impacts lifetime earnings **more than your college degree**
- CareerBuilder: **70% of hiring managers** expect candidates to negotiate salary — yet most shy away
- Harvard Business School: 15 minutes of daily reflection → **23% better performance** than peers
- Eric Schmidt: "If you're offered a seat on a rocket ship, **don't ask which seat**"
- Angela Duckworth: consultant → teacher → researcher → professor → "Grit" bestseller at **mid-40s**; Jim McKelvey: glass blower → co-founded Square at **mid-40s**

**The Seven Moves:**

| # | Move | Core Idea | Key Action |
|---|------|-----------|------------|
| 1 | Pick Right Industry | Industry choice > college degree for lifetime earnings | Industry fit audit: growing > GDP? Rewards innovation? Employees share upside? Clear paths? AI impact? |
| 2 | Specialist vs Generalist | Phase 1 (0-5yr): build depth; Phase 2 (5-10yr): fork; Phase 3: accelerate; avoid "career fog zone" | Talk to someone 5-10 years ahead on each path; some careers are tunnels, others are bridges |
| 3 | RPM Engine | Reputation (trusted under high stakes) + Production (10x value) + Mastery (the extra 1%) | Weekly: Who did I help? What extra value did I create? What did I learn? |
| 4 | Get Close to Customer | Proximity to customer = leverage; every dollar traces back to the customer's decision to buy | Map how your role impacts the customer; find one project that gets you closer to revenue |
| 5 | Asymmetric Bets | High upside, manageable downside; took $20K pay cut → career-defining opportunity | 2x2 matrix: worst case? Recover in 12 months? Best case? Upside ≥ 5x downside? |
| 6 | Master Financial Forest | Salary fills drip by drip; investments and equity make it overflow | Don't act rich early; automate savings first; negotiate everything (salary + equity) |
| 7 | Meditate to Monetize | 15 min daily reflection = 23% better performance | End of each day: What moved forward? What did I learn? What matters most tomorrow? |

**Key Takeaways:**
1. Career evolution model: Foundation (0-5yr, build depth) → Fork (5-10yr, specialist or generalist) → Accelerate; avoid the mushy middle "career fog zone"
2. RPM: Reputation ("20 years to build, 5 minutes to ruin" — Buffett), Production (create $10M value to earn $1M), Mastery (1,000 contacts with the ball daily like Tiger Woods)
3. Uber IPO story: a managing director drove as an Uber driver to understand customers → bank won $40M deal; proximity to customer is power
4. Asymmetric bet framework: worst case manageable? Recovery < 12 months? Upside ≥ 5x downside? If yes → take the bet
5. Financial principles: don't upgrade lifestyle when income jumps (stayed in same apartment, same Honda Civic); save first, spend what's left; learn equity terms (ESOP, RSU, ISO, vesting, strike price)

## Story

Two cousins, Lok and Yin, graduated from the same university in Hong Kong in the same year. Both studied engineering. Both were sharp. But they made very different choices.

Lok took a job at a traditional manufacturing company. Good salary, stable benefits, clear hierarchy. Yin took a job at a mid-sized AI startup. Slightly lower starting pay, but the industry was growing faster than GDP, rewarded innovation, and gave employees equity.

Five years later, Lok had gotten steady raises. Yin's base salary was only a bit higher. But Yin owned stock options in a company that had tripled in value. The difference was not talent. It was the industry. Georgetown research showed that industry choice impacts lifetime earnings more than your college degree. Lok built on a solid but slow-moving foundation. Yin picked the rocket ship. As Eric Schmidt once said, "If you are offered a seat on a rocket ship, do not ask which seat."

Around year five, both cousins hit a fork. Lok had to decide: go deeper into hardware testing (specialist) or move into project management (generalist). Yin faced the same crossroads in AI -- stay in computer vision research or shift into leading a product team. Their uncle, a retired engineer in his sixties, warned them about the "career fog zone" -- the mushy middle where you are not deep enough to be an expert and not broad enough to be a leader. "Talk to someone who is five to ten years ahead on each path," he said. "Some careers are tunnels. Others are bridges."

Yin chose the specialist path -- deeper into CV research. Lok went generalist, moving into operations. Neither choice was wrong. What mattered was that they chose deliberately instead of drifting.

At work, Yin ran what she called her RPM engine -- three gauges she checked every Friday. Reputation: "Who did I help this week? Am I the person people trust when stakes are high?" Warren Buffett's line rang in her head: "It takes twenty years to build a reputation and five minutes to ruin it." Production: "Did I create ten times more value than I cost?" To earn a million, you need to create ten million in value. Mastery: "What is the extra one percent I practiced that nobody saw?" Like Tiger Woods hitting a thousand practice shots before a tournament.

The biggest turning point came when Yin volunteered for a customer-facing project. She was a researcher, comfortable behind her models and datasets. But she remembered a story about an Uber IPO: a managing director at an investment bank actually signed up to drive as an Uber driver for a week to understand the customer experience. That insight helped the bank win a forty-million-dollar deal. Every dollar traces back to the customer's decision to buy. Yin spent two weeks sitting in on client demos. She saw how her models were actually used. She redesigned a feature that cut false positives by thirty percent -- not because she got smarter, but because she got closer.

Then came the bet. Another startup offered Yin a role. The pay was twenty thousand dollars less. But the upside was massive: early equity in a company entering a white-hot market. She ran the asymmetric bet framework. Worst case? She takes a pay cut for a year and the startup folds. Can she recover in twelve months? Yes. Best case? The equity is worth five times or more what she gave up. She took the bet. It was the career-defining move she almost talked herself out of.

Through all of this, Yin kept one financial principle sacred: do not upgrade your lifestyle when your income jumps. She stayed in the same apartment. She drove the same car. She automated her savings so the money moved before she could spend it. And she negotiated -- not just salary, but equity. Seventy percent of hiring managers expect candidates to negotiate. Most people never do.

Every evening, Yin blocked fifteen minutes at the end of her day. She asked herself three questions: What moved forward today? What did I learn? What matters most tomorrow? Harvard Business School found that fifteen minutes of daily reflection leads to twenty-three percent better performance than peers who skip it. It was not meditation in the spiritual sense. It was meditation that monetized -- turning raw experience into compounding insight.

Lok, by the way, did fine. Steady career, comfortable life. But when the cousins met for dinner on Lok's fortieth birthday, the gap was undeniable. Not just in money. In options. In freedom. In the ability to say no to things that did not matter.

**The lesson:** Wealth as an employee is built by picking the right industry, choosing your specialist or generalist path deliberately, running your RPM engine weekly, getting close to the customer, taking asymmetric bets where the upside dwarfs the downside, saving before spending, and reflecting for fifteen minutes every day -- because the best compound interest is the kind that runs on your own growth.
