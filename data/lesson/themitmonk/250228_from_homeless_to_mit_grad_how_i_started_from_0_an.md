## Summary

Twelve business insights plus a bonus: **solve problems (not missions), chase problems (not passion), success = subtraction, evidence over ego, sell promises (not products), watch customers (don't just listen), founder-market fit, product-market fit, pricing experiments, hire for adaptability, build processes (OKRs + rituals), cash is oxygen, and lead with love** — people → product → profits, in that order.

**Why it matters:**
- Microsoft started writing software for a computer company that no longer exists; Facebook started as a "hot or not" site — no grand missions
- Bourbon (check-in app) pivoted in 2 months to photo sharing → became **Instagram**
- Freemium startups: 90% retention rate **plummeted to 10%** when they started charging — they never had product-market fit
- SVB swept **$10M** from a company's bank account overnight due to a covenant breach — revenue/profit is accounting; **only cash is real**
- A search engine company pivoted from B2C to B2B → acquired by **Microsoft for $1.2B**

**The Twelve Insights + Bonus:**

| # | Insight | Core Idea | Key Example |
|---|---------|-----------|-------------|
| 1 | Solve Problems, Not Missions | Mission statements don't build businesses; solving real problems does | Microsoft: 2 guys writing software; Theranos/FTX: inspiring missions → prison |
| 2 | Chase Problems, Not Passion | Passions don't last; problems do | Airbnb: couldn't afford hotel; search engine → Yahoo → Microsoft $1.2B pivot |
| 3 | Success = Subtraction | Big wins from doing fewer things better | Southwest: subtracted meals/seats; Apple: Jobs cut all product lines to survive |
| 4 | Evidence Over Ego | Data > intuition; AB test everything | Pricing experiment: expected model A or B → data showed model C (volume-based) |
| 5 | Sell Promises, Not Products | Customers buy better versions of themselves | Nike beat Adidas by selling a dream (Air Jordans); Ziploc = reducing waste feeling |
| 6 | Watch, Don't Just Listen | Customers say one thing, do another | Ford: "faster horses"; AI fraud company: prospects praised but didn't buy → different targeting |
| 7 | Founder-Market Fit | Best founders ARE their market | Melanie Perkins: design instructor → built Canva from firsthand pain |
| 8 | Product-Market Fit | Without PMF, no business; be scrappy, watch cash | Bourbon → Instagram: users shared photos not check-ins; pivoted in 2 months |
| 9 | Pricing Experiments | Free isn't a business model; test willingness to pay early | 90% retention → 10% when charging = no PMF; find the right price point |
| 10 | Hire for Adaptability | Every company changes direction in 18 months | Pay top dollar, give equity, make them owners; find people who roll with punches |
| 11 | Build Processes | OKRs + communication rituals = speed | Daily standups, weekly 1:1s, Monday exec meetings, all-hands; 3-4 OKRs/quarter |
| 12 | Cash Is Oxygen | Revenue ≠ cash; profit ≠ cash; only cash is real | SVB swept $10M overnight; CFO didn't track cash; company never recovered |
| Bonus | Lead With Love | People → product → profits, in that order | Be a coach, not a boss; your people take care of customers who take care of competition |

**Key Takeaways:**
1. "Solve a problem, nail a product, and the mission will follow" — Theranos and FTX had inspiring missions; founders are in prison; Microsoft and Facebook had no grand mission at birth
2. Success is subtraction: Southwest subtracted meals, reserved seats, big airport reps; Steve Jobs was weeks from bankruptcy and cut almost all product lines to focus on iMac
3. "Everybody lies" — AI fraud company: every prospect praised the product but nobody bought; switched to high-urgency customers → explosive growth
4. "Revenue is not cash. Profit is not cash. Only cash is real. The rest is accounting." — SVB swept $10M overnight from a company's bank account; cash is vital oxygen
5. "Lead with love: take care of your people, your product, and your profits — in that order. When you take care of your people, your people take care of your customers, and your customers take care of your competition."

## Story

Imagine two engineers leaving their corporate jobs to start AI companies in a Hong Kong science park, both with PhDs, both with families, both betting their savings on building something from scratch.

Insights 1-2 — Problems, Not Missions: Fung spends months crafting a perfect mission statement: "Democratizing AI for legal innovation across Asia-Pacific." Beautiful words, no customer. Mei solves a problem she experienced herself — while doing R&D for a small trademark detection startup, she watched law firm partners spend hours manually comparing visual trademarks against registry databases. The pain was obvious. Like Airbnb's founders who couldn't afford a hotel room, Mei builds for a problem she's lived with for five years. Fung's company has an inspiring "why" but no product-market fit. Mei's has a real customer pain that keeps her partner firms working until midnight. Solve a problem first, and the mission will follow.

Insights 3-4 — Subtraction and Evidence: Fung adds every feature law firms request — document management, billing integration, court filing automation, case timelines. His product becomes bloated and nobody uses it well. Mei subtracts — like Southwest cutting meals and reserved seats to build a giant business, she offers exactly one thing: visual trademark similarity search with automated conflict reports. Three features. That's it. When it comes to pricing, Fung goes with his gut: "Enterprise AI should be premium." Mei runs experiments — she expected per-search pricing to win, but data showed that a flat monthly subscription with unlimited searches is what firms actually want. Evidence over ego. Stop guessing.

Insights 5-6 — Selling Dreams and Watching: Fung sells features in his demo: "Our model uses transformer-based embeddings with cosine similarity in a high-dimensional vector space." Partners' eyes glaze. Mei sells promises: "Your junior associates will stop spending three hours on manual searches and start spending that time on analysis that only humans can do — you're buying back their judgment time." Like Nike beating Adidas not with better shoes but by selling the Air Jordan dream. When three firms praise Mei's demo but don't sign, she doesn't celebrate — she watches. What are they actually doing after the demo? Nothing. She shifts her focus to firms with active infringement disputes — high urgency, immediate pain. Two sign within a week. "What they do speaks louder than what they say."

Insights 7-8 — Founder-Market Fit and PMF: Mei IS her market — like Melanie Perkins, a design instructor who built Canva from her own pain. Mei has walked in the examiners' shoes. She knows which reports they trust, which formats waste their time, and which edge cases (composite marks, color variations) keep them up at night. When law firms start using her similarity reports not just for infringement disputes but for pre-filing trademark clearance — a use case she didn't design for — she pivots in two months. Like Bourbon's check-in app discovering users cared about photo sharing and becoming Instagram, Mei follows the signal. The clearance product takes off. Fung tries freemium: 90% of firms use his free tier. He starts charging: retention drops to 10%. He never had PMF.

Insights 9-12 — Scaling: Mei prices through experiments, not guesses. She hires for adaptability — every startup she's been at changed direction within 18 months, so she looks for engineers who've pivoted before. She pays the best salary she can afford, gives equity, and makes them owners. She builds lightweight processes for an eight-person team: 10-minute daily standups, weekly 1:1s, Monday planning, and three OKRs per quarter cascading from company to individual. She obsesses over cash — not revenue, not profit. "Only cash is real. The rest is accounting." When a law firm delays payment by 60 days and another cancels a contract, she knows exactly how many weeks of runway remain because she tracks cash weekly. Cash is oxygen — especially when you have a family depending on you and half your personal salary goes to living expenses.

Bonus — Lead With Love: Mei's approach to her tiny team is controversial for a startup: people → product → profits, in that order. She's a coach, not a boss. When the junior developer makes a mistake that ships a wrong batch of reports to a client, she doesn't fire him — she sits with him, reviews the root cause together, and they build a validation check so it can't happen again. Her people become fearless because they know mistakes are for learning, not punishment. They take care of clients. Clients take care of referrals. That's how an eight-person startup in a science park starts competing with companies ten times their size.

**The lesson:** Solve problems, not missions — Microsoft had no grand mission at the start; Theranos had a beautiful one and the founders went to prison. Chase problems you've lived with, not abstract passions. Success is subtraction — fewer features, done better. Evidence beats ego — test pricing, test messaging, test everything. Sell promises, not features — law firms buy time back, not embeddings. Watch customers, don't just listen — follow what they do, not what they praise. Find founder-market fit (walk in their shoes) and product-market fit (pivot fast when usage shows you the real product). Price through experiments. Hire for adaptability. Build processes even at eight people. Cash is the only thing that's real. And lead with love — people first, always.
