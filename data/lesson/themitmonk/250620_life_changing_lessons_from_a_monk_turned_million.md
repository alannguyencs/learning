## Summary

Seven monk-to-CEO lessons: **rock bottom is a foundation, feed the right wolf (FIRED framework), self-reflection as superpower, 3-minute mentors, to-BE list > to-do list, stop comparing, and the musk deer parable** — you may already carry what you're searching for.

**Why it matters:**
- By the time you finish a sentence, **23 million of your cells** have been replaced — everything is short-lived, including setbacks
- Our brain is literally **wired to obsess** over mistakes and failures (reptilian brain) — negativity is the default wolf
- Tying habits to **identity** ("I want to be healthy") is more motivating than outcomes ("I want to lose 20 pounds") — James Clear's research
- Pew Research: **25% of teenage girls** say social media hurts their mental health; **45% of teens** say it hurts sleep
- Scored **34 out of 700** on finals → a 3-minute conversation with a friend changed everything → master's program with full scholarship

**The Seven Lessons:**

| # | Lesson | Core Idea | Key Action |
|---|--------|-----------|------------|
| 1 | Rock Bottom = Foundation | Setbacks are launching pads, not endpoints; everything is impermanent | Trick your brain: breakthrough is inevitable; pivot freely |
| 2 | Feed the Right Wolf | Negative wolf is fed by default (reptilian brain); aim for positive 51% of the time | FIRED: catch Fraud, Identity, Reading minds, Exaggeration, Dooming thoughts |
| 3 | Self-Reflection | Can't change what you can't see; meditation = clearing the cache | Start 5 min/day; train attention, not emptiness; observe which wolf gets fed |
| 4 | 3-Minute Mentors | One person believing in you for 3 minutes can change your trajectory | Put yourself in learning environments; ask people about their journey |
| 5 | To-BE List | Identity > outcomes; you control how you show up, not how the world shows up | Every morning: write one word for how you want to show up today |
| 6 | Stop Comparing | Elite performers care about one thing: better today than yesterday | Ask: "What am I better at today than last month?"; build a personal scoreboard |
| 7 | Musk Deer | You may already carry what you're frantically searching for | Pause before chasing external validation; "Am I running through the forest looking for something I already carry?" |

**Key Takeaways:**
1. "There's a crack in everything. That's how the light gets in." — Leonard Cohen; rock bottom = solid ground = foundation to build from
2. FIRED framework to catch negative thoughts: Fraud ("I'm fake"), Identity ("I'm lazy"), Reading minds ("they don't like me"), Exaggeration ("I always mess up"), Dooming ("I'll never recover")
3. Meditation = clearing the computer cache; alpha/beta/theta/gamma brain waves reset; self-reflection is your superpower
4. To-BE list connects to everything: it helps complete to-do lists and to-have lists; "patient, curious, present, helpful" — one word each morning
5. The musk deer searches everywhere for a scent that comes from within — maybe happiness creates success, not the other way around

## Story

Kevin ran a small electronics repair shop in Sham Shui Po. At thirty-five, with a wife and a toddler at home, he thought he had it figured out. Then a water pipe burst overnight, destroying half his inventory. Insurance covered almost nothing. He sat on the wet floor of his shop at 2 a.m., staring at a pile of ruined circuit boards, and thought, "This is the end."

His landlord, Mrs. Cheung, came by the next morning. She had run a noodle stall for forty years and survived two typhoons, a rent tripling, and a pandemic. She looked at the mess and said, "Good. Now you know what the floor feels like. You can only build from the floor -- you cannot build from mid-air."

That was Lesson 1: rock bottom is not a grave. It is a foundation.

Over the next few weeks, Kevin noticed something. Every time he sat down to plan rebuilding, a voice in his head whispered: "You are a fraud. Nobody will come back. You always mess things up." He started writing these thoughts on sticky notes -- not to dwell on them, but to label them. "Fraud." "Exaggeration." "Dooming." Mrs. Cheung had told him about an old parable: two wolves live inside you, one dark and one bright, and the one you feed is the one that grows. Kevin did not try to kill the dark wolf. He just tried to feed the bright one slightly more often -- 51 percent of the time, not 100.

That was Lesson 2: the FIRED framework. Name the negative thought (Fraud, Identity, Reading minds, Exaggeration, Dooming) and you weaken its grip.

He started spending five minutes each morning sitting quietly before his son woke up -- not trying to empty his mind, just noticing which wolf was getting fed that day. It was like clearing the cache on an old computer. The system ran smoother afterward.

That was Lesson 3: self-reflection is a superpower.

One afternoon a customer, a retired university professor, stayed to chat for three minutes while waiting for her phone repair. She told Kevin she had failed her doctoral qualifying exam twice before passing. "The people who believed in me for just a few minutes changed the direction of my entire life," she said. Kevin realized that one short conversation with a person who sees your potential can bend your whole trajectory.

That was Lesson 4: three-minute mentors.

Kevin started each morning by writing one word on a sticky note above his workbench. Not a task. A word for how he wanted to show up that day. "Patient." "Curious." "Present." He noticed something strange: the to-do list took care of itself when the to-be word was clear. If the word was "patient," he handled difficult customers better, which led to better reviews, which brought in more business. Identity drove outcomes, not the other way around.

That was Lesson 5: a to-be list beats a to-do list.

He also stopped scrolling through Instagram pages of flashy repair channels with millions of followers. Instead, every Sunday he asked himself one question: "What am I better at today than last month?" He tracked skills gained, not followers earned. His personal scoreboard replaced everyone else's highlight reel.

That was Lesson 6: stop comparing.

Months later, the shop was busier than ever. One evening, Kevin caught himself thinking about expanding to a second location, chasing the next milestone. Then he remembered Mrs. Cheung telling him the story of the musk deer -- a creature that runs frantically through the forest searching for a beautiful scent, never realizing the scent comes from its own body. Kevin paused. He looked at his small shop, his loyal customers, his son's drawing taped to the register. Maybe he was already carrying what he had been searching for all along.

**The lesson:** Rock bottom is a foundation, not a finish line -- feed the bright wolf just 51 percent of the time, reflect daily, seek three-minute mentors, choose who to be before what to do, compare only to yesterday-you, and remember that you may already carry what you are searching for.
